About

I am a PhD student in neuroeconomics in Christian Ruff’s group at the University of Zurich. My broad interests are resource-rational cognition, noisy inference, efficient coding, and Bayesian decoding—how a constrained brain exploits environmental structure to compute efficiently, yielding systematic biases and variability in behavior.

Much of my work uses this perspective to explain well-known biases in economic decision making. For example, I study how noisy and efficient computations can produce probability distortions in described risk—see a recent preprint here. Related projects extend this view to the description–experience gap in decisions under risk, and to how task goals modulate computations in multi-attribute choice. Ongoing work examines valuation as a modular, sequential, resource-rational noisy inference process.

To date, my focus has been on single-trial decisions. I am now extending the same framework to sequential decision-making, where the temporal structure of trials and task goals shape planning and learning in more complex settings. Happy to chat.
