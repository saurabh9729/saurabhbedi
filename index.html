About

I am a PhD student in neuroeconomics in Christian Ruff’s group at the University of Zurich. My broad research interests are in understanding how a resource-constrained brain exploits the structure of the world to compute efficiently according to the goals of the agent, and how this approach yields systematic biases and variability patterns seen in human behavior.

For example, I study how noisy inference of bounded quantities like probabilities, with either Bayes-optimal decoding or efficient encoding can independently produce the classic probability distortion pattern from prospect theory (see preprint). Related projects use similar resource-rational formalisms to explain the description–experience gap in probability distortions, to show how task goals modulate computations in multi-attribute decisions, and to model valuation as a higher-order sequential (resource limited) inference process.

Interests

While my work so far has focused on simple, single-trial decisions, I am now moving toward sequential decision making—again asking how resource-constrained computations, informed by both environmental structure and task goals, can explain behavior. This opens up a way of understanding more complex phenomena such as planning and learning under the same principles. I think this is a useful and exciting direction.

As a side interest, I also enjoy thinking about how these abstract cognitive computations might emerge as properties of statistical physical systems (e.g., the Ising model). While orthogonal to my main research, these loosely formed connections often inspire my thinking about how such computations could be implemented.
